Lab of Advanced Biomaterials and Nanomedicine

Ⅰ.Mechanism-Informed Combinatorial Immunotherapy

Prof. Zhuang Liu & Qian Chen 2019-2022

  • Refined immunotherapy for tumor radiofrequency ablation (RFA) via temperature sensitive hydrogel (thermogel)
    Project HostJune. 2020-Aug. 2021

    In this research, a convenient local administration strategy by blocking rho-associated kinases (ROCK) is applied to amplify the immune responses triggered by RFA via promoting the phagocytosis capacity of DCs. Briefly, ROCK inhibitors were successfully dispersed in amphiphilic copolymer poly(D,L-lactide-co-glycolide)-b-poly(ethylene glycol)-b-poly(D,L-lactideco-glycolide) (PLGA-PEG-PLGA) aqueous solution, which is a sol at room temperature and forms hydrogel quickly at body temperature, successfully prolonging the retention of ROCK inhibitors after just one single injection. Interestingly, in the melanoma tumor model, the generated tumor fragments after RFA treatment were swallowed by DCs and underwent reinforced antigen presentation process with the help of released ROCK inhibitors, further effectively activating T cell-mediated anti-tumor immune responses and significantly improving the therapeutic efficiency of RFA. Moreover, such strategy remarkably prolongs the survival of mice after RFA treatment, showing great potential for clinical translation as an improvement strategy for RFA.


    Achieved Goals
    • Increased maturation of DCs
    • Enhanced phagocytosis of DCs
    • Activation of T cell-mediated anti-tumor immune responses

  • Self-assembled anti-inflammatory hydrogel for enhanced αPDL1 immune checkpoint therapy
    Project Cooperator Apr.2019-May.2020

    Here, an anti-inflammatory nanofiber hydrogel self-assembled by betamethasone (steroid drug) is developed for local delivery of anti-programmed cell death protein ligand 1 (αPDL1). On the one hand, this carrier-free system based on steroid drugs can reprogram the pro-tumoral immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) to anti-tumoral TME via inhibiting NF-κB signal pathway; on the other hand, it would serve as a reservoir for sustained release of αPDL1 so as to synergistically boost the immune system. By local injection of such αPDL1 loaded hydrogel, therapeutic effects were observed in inhibiting both local tumors and abscopal tumors without any treatment. This work presents a unique hydrogel-based delivery system using clinically approved drugs, showing promise in improving the objective response rate of immune checkpoint therapy (ICB) and minimizing its systemic toxicity.

  • Mechanobiological and immune regulative hydrogel (PNIPAm-P) for wound closure
    Project Cooperator Oct.2020-Mar.2021

    Here, a mechanically active adhesive and immune regulative dressing is developed, mainly consisting of temperature-sensitive hydrogel [poly(N-isopropylacrylamide) PNIPAm] with semi-interpenetrating network structure. This hydrogel could strongly adhere to and actively contract the wound. More interestingly, the interspersed [poly(methacrylic acid)PMAA] could further promote wound healing via increasing the formation of new blood vessels and modulating inflammatory responses. In vivo mouse and porcine wound models validate the accelerated wound healing enabled by active contraction and modulated inflammatory responses. This mechanobiological and immune regulative dressing may provide a new avenue for wound management.

  • Injectable Reactive Oxygen Species-Responsive SN38 Prodrug Scaffold with Checkpoint Inhibitors for Combined Chemoimmunotherapy
    Project Cooperator Oct. 2020-Mar. 2021

    Here, the reactive oxygen species (ROS)-responsive hydrogel, covalently cross-linked by phenylboronic acid-modified 7-ethyl-10-hydroxycamptothecin (SN38- SA−BA) with poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA), is fabricated for topical delivery of anti-programmed cell death protein ligand 1 antibodies (aPDL1). In the presence of endogenous ROS, SN38-SA−BA will be oxidized and hydrolyzed, leading to the degradation of hydrogel and the release of initial free SN38 and encapsulated aPDL1. It is demonstrated that SN38 could elicit specific immune responses by triggering immunogenic cell death (ICD) of cancer cells, a distinct cell death pathway featured with the release of immunostimulatory damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). Meanwhile, the released aPDL1 could bind to programmed cell death protein ligand 1 (PDL1) expressed on cancer cells to augment antitumor T cell responses. Thus, the ROS-responsive prodrug hydrogel loaded with aPDL1 could induce effective innate and adaptive antitumor immune responses after local injection, significantly inhibiting or even eliminating those tumors.
